BANGALORE

Bangalore, officially known as Bengaluru, is one of India’s most dynamic cities, celebrated for its blend of tradition and modernity. Its history stretches back more than a thousand years, with layers of political, cultural, and economic change shaping it into the “Silicon Valley of India” we know today. The origins of Bangalore can be traced to the 9th and 10th centuries, when the region formed part of the Ganga dynasty’s territories. Inscriptions from this era, found in areas such as Begur, refer to the place as “Bengaval-uru,” meaning “City of Guards” in Kannada, hinting at its early strategic importance. Over time, the name evolved into Bengaluru. In popular legend, however, the name’s origin is attributed to a 12th-century incident involving Hoysala king Veera Ballala II, who, lost during a hunting expedition, was offered a humble meal of boiled beans by a poor woman. Grateful for the hospitality, he named the place “Benda-Kaal-ooru” — “town of boiled beans” — which, over centuries, phonetically morphed into Bangalore. During the medieval period, Bangalore was a small fortified settlement surrounded by fertile lands and dotted with villages. The 14th century saw the rise of the Vijayanagara Empire, which brought stability and economic growth to the region. Under the Vijayanagara rulers, Bangalore developed as a trade center for agricultural produce, textiles, and handicrafts. The real transformation began in 1537, when Kempegowda I, a feudal chieftain under the Vijayanagara kings, built a mud fort and laid the foundations of the modern city. Kempegowda is credited with designing Bangalore’s layout, demarcating areas for different trades, and constructing temples and water tanks (kere) that still exist in some form today. His visionary town planning, with its market areas and civic structures, gave Bangalore its first organized urban identity. After the decline of the Vijayanagara Empire in the late 16th century, Bangalore came under the control of various rulers. It was briefly under the Bijapur Sultanate before being captured by the Marathas, and later, in the late 17th century, by the Mughal Empire. The Mughals, in turn, sold Bangalore to Chikkadevaraja Wodeyar, the ruler of Mysore, for 3 lakh rupees in 1687. The Wodeyars ruled the region for decades, contributing to its cultural heritage. In the mid-18th century, Bangalore became a focal point of conflict between the Mysore rulers Hyder Ali and Tipu Sultan and the British East India Company. Hyder Ali recognized Bangalore’s strategic location and strengthened its defenses, building a stone fort to replace Kempegowda’s mud fort. Tipu Sultan, his son, continued to modernize the city, introducing better administration and fostering trade links with foreign countries. The Anglo-Mysore Wars (1767–1799) brought Bangalore into the spotlight of colonial ambitions. In 1791, during the Third Anglo-Mysore War, British forces under Lord Cornwallis captured Bangalore after a fierce siege. Following Tipu Sultan’s defeat and death in 1799, Bangalore returned to Wodeyar rule, but the British retained a strong presence in the region. In 1831, citing administrative inefficiency, the British took over direct control of Bangalore from the Mysore kings and made it the administrative capital of the Mysore state. The British established the Bangalore Cantonment, distinct from the old “Pettah” (native town), and built wide roads, churches, bungalows, and parade grounds. This dual-city structure — the Pettah with its traditional Indian character and the Cantonment with its colonial architecture and cosmopolitan culture — became a hallmark of 19th-century Bangalore. The British era was transformative. Bangalore’s salubrious climate, green landscapes, and clean environment made it a preferred location for the British to station troops and build residences. Infrastructure improvements included the introduction of telegraph services, railways (linking Bangalore to Madras in 1864), and piped water supply from the Hesaraghatta and later Thippagondanahalli reservoirs. Educational institutions like Bishop Cotton Boys’ School (1865), St. Joseph’s College (1882), and the Indian Institute of Science (1909, founded with support from industrialist Jamshedji Tata and the Mysore Maharaja) laid the groundwork for the city’s later reputation as a hub of learning and research. In the 20th century, Bangalore played an important role in India’s freedom movement. Nationalist leaders such as Mahatma Gandhi visited the city multiple times, and local activists organized protests, boycotts, and public meetings. After India’s independence in 1947, Bangalore became the capital of the Mysore state (renamed Karnataka in 1973). The city began to expand rapidly, driven by state-led industrialization. Public sector undertakings like Hindustan Aeronautics Limited (HAL), Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL), and Hindustan Machine Tools (HMT) established their headquarters here, turning Bangalore into a center for aerospace, defense production, and precision engineering. The post-1990s era marked another turning point, as Bangalore emerged as the epicenter of India’s information technology (IT) boom. Liberalization policies, combined with Bangalore’s skilled English-speaking workforce and strong educational base, attracted major IT companies like Infosys, Wipro, and later global tech giants such as IBM, Microsoft, and Google. The city’s reputation as a technology hub earned it the nickname “Silicon Valley of India.” The IT boom transformed Bangalore’s economy, skyline, and demographics, bringing in migrants from across India and even from abroad. At the same time, it also created challenges in terms of infrastructure, traffic congestion, and environmental sustainability. In 2006, the city’s official name was restored to “Bengaluru,” aligning with its Kannada heritage, though “Bangalore” remains widely used. Today, the city is a blend of the old and new — from centuries-old temples like Dodda Basavana Gudi and Someshwara Temple to modern landmarks like the Bengaluru International Airport and high-tech parks. Its rich cultural scene includes classical music, theatre, literature, and cuisine, while its gardens and lakes — though under pressure from urbanization — still echo the city’s earlier reputation as the “Garden City.” Bangalore’s history is thus a tapestry woven from many influences — ancient dynasties, medieval chieftains, sultans, colonial administrators, freedom fighters, industrialists, and tech entrepreneurs. It has evolved from a fortified market town to a garrison city, from an industrial base to a global IT powerhouse. Yet, through all these changes, it retains a distinctive character: cosmopolitan yet rooted in local traditions, innovative yet respectful of heritage. The story of Bangalore is not just a chronicle of political events and economic milestones, but also a reflection of resilience and adaptability — qualities that will likely define its future as much as they have shaped its past. ---
